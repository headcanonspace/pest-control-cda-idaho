Mice and Rats We Commonly Treat

Most calls we get are about one of two things, house mice or Norway rats, and they're easier to tell apart than people think.

House mice are small, usually gray or light brown, and they're the ones getting into pantries, drawers, and the backs of cupboards. Norway rats are bigger, they tend to stick to basements, crawl spaces, and areas near woodpiles or trash, and their droppings are noticeably larger too.

First Signs of a Rodent Problem

Once you know what you're dealing with, the next step is spotting it early, before it turns into a bigger problem. Here's what to check for.

  • Droppings: small and dark, usually found in a pantry, under the sink, or behind the stove
  • Gnaw marks: on food packaging, cardboard boxes, or chewed corners on wood and wiring
  • Scratching in the walls or attic: especially at night, since mice and rats are most active after dark
  • Disturbed insulation: pulled apart or flattened in a line, usually in the attic or crawl space

How Mice and Rats Get Into Your Home

Most homes look sealed from the outside, but mice and rats find their way in anyway, especially as it starts cooling off in fall and they're looking for somewhere warm. A mouse only needs a gap about the size of a coin to squeeze through, and rats can gnaw through weaker materials like thin wood or plastic to make an opening bigger. The usual entry points are smaller than people expect, cracks in the foundation, gaps around pipes or vents, and edges around garage doors that have worn down over time. Once inside, they'll often follow the same path through the walls or insulation to get from one part of the house to another.

Is It Dangerous to Have Mice or Rats in the House?

Yes, it's a real health risk, not just an inconvenience. Droppings and urine can carry bacteria that contaminate food, counters, and stored items, especially in a kitchen or pantry. The risk isn't from seeing a mouse, it's from what's left behind in places you touch or cook, which is why cleaning up after an infestation matters as much as getting rid of the rodents themselves.

What Can I Do to Prevent Rodent Infestations?

Sealing the entry points during a service is a strong start, but a few habits at home help keep it that way.

  • Seal small gaps and cracks: check around pipes, vents, and the foundation regularly, since even a small gap left open can be an opening again
  • Store food in sealed containers: this includes pantry items and pet food, loose bags are an easy target
  • Keep the yard clear: woodpiles, debris, and overgrown brush near the house give rodents a place to hide before they try to get inside
  • Don't leave pet food out overnight: bring bowls in after your pet's done eating, especially outdoors

Deer Mice and Hantavirus Information

Deer mice are found throughout parts of Idaho and can carry Hantavirus Pulmonary Syndrome (HPS). If you discover rodent droppings or nesting materials inside a cabin, garage, shed, or other enclosed structure, avoid sweeping or vacuuming the area, as this may disturb contaminated dust. Follow appropriate cleaning precautions and consider having the property inspected before cleanup.
